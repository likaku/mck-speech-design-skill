# Tone & Style Guide for Speech Writing

## Tone Spectrum

Select the appropriate tone based on the speaker's persona and event context.

### Formal-Professional
- **When**: Board meetings, regulatory presentations, investor briefings
- **Markers**: Complete sentences, no contractions, third-person references, measured pace
- **Example**: "The data indicates a 23% improvement in operational efficiency over the preceding fiscal quarter."

### Executive-Conversational
- **When**: C-suite exchanges, partner meetings, executive roundtables
- **Markers**: First-person "we/I", rhetorical questions, strategic framing, data + narrative blend
- **Example**: "Here's what the data actually shows — and it surprised us too. Volume is up 4.4%, but average price is down 3.4%. They're buying more, paying less. That's not recession behavior — that's value optimization."

### Thought-Leadership
- **When**: Conferences, keynotes, industry panels
- **Markers**: Bold statements, frameworks/models, pattern recognition, forward-looking claims
- **Example**: "Every market has an underserved 'lower tier.' China just happens to be demonstrating this at the most visible scale."

### Collaborative-Workshop
- **When**: Working sessions, co-creation, internal team meetings
- **Markers**: Inclusive "we/us", questions inviting input, building on others' points
- **Example**: "Let's break this down together. What patterns are you seeing in your own markets?"

### Inspirational-Motivational
- **When**: Company all-hands, award ceremonies, launch events
- **Markers**: Short punchy sentences, repetition for rhythm, emotional storytelling, vision-painting
- **Example**: "Three years ago, this was an idea on a whiteboard. Today, it's running in production for 16 million users a day."

## Trust-Building Techniques

### Transparency Over Promises
- NEVER: "We guarantee..." / "We won't let you down" / "We promise results"
- INSTEAD: "The track record shows..." / "Here's what we've observed..." / "Whether this maps to your context is worth exploring"

### Acknowledge Limitations
- "Is our global cloud as mature as AWS? In certain categories, honestly, no."
- "Is this the only approach? Of course not. But it's the framework behind the results we showed."
- "Not everything that works in China works elsewhere."

### Let Data Speak
- Lead with numbers, follow with interpretation
- "The data suggests..." not "We believe..."
- Cite specific sources, percentages, named cases

### Self-Q&A Pattern
Pose questions the audience is likely thinking, then answer them. This creates engagement and demonstrates empathy.

| Pattern | When to Use |
|---------|------------|
| "You might be wondering: [question]" | Introducing a new topic |
| "The natural follow-up question is..." | After presenting a data point |
| "Fair question. Here's what the data says." | Acknowledging potential skepticism |
| "Here's a question worth sitting with: [provocative question]" | Before an important insight |
| "So the question becomes..." | Pivoting from data to interpretation |
| "Now, is that because [generous interpretation]? Honestly, [real reason]." | Building credibility through candor |

## Writing the Script

### Sentence Structure
- Spoken sentences should be shorter than written ones: 10-20 words average
- Use em-dashes for natural pauses: "The answer — and it surprised us — is..."
- Front-load the key information: "17x growth. That's what online GMV did in six years."
- Vary rhythm: short declarative statement, then a longer explanatory one

### Vocabulary
- Match the audience's vocabulary, not the speaker's
- Define jargon on first use: "private domain — what we mean is the direct consumer relationship the brand owns"
- Avoid acronym dumps: spell out first, then use acronym
- Use concrete language over abstractions: "1.4 billion users" not "massive user base"

### Bridging Techniques for Smooth Delivery
- Callback: "Remember when I mentioned X? Here's why."
- Contrast: "That was the challenge. Now let's talk about the solution."
- Question: "So what happens next?"
- Numbers: "Three points. First..."

### Emphasis Markers in Script
Use **bold** for words the speaker should stress. Use *italics* for words with emphasis on contrast or nuance. Use `[pause]` for deliberate pauses.

Example:
> The number-one conversion driver — by a **massive** margin — is **exclusive products and limited drops, at 77%**. *Not* price. Price matching is only 22%. `[pause]` Think about what that means.

## Common Pitfalls

1. **Over-promising**: Statements the speaker can't back up erode trust instantly
2. **Jargon without context**: Alienates audience members unfamiliar with terms
3. **Too many numbers**: More than 3 data points per slide causes cognitive overload
4. **Reading the slides**: Script should complement slides, not duplicate them
5. **No transitions**: Abrupt topic shifts lose the audience
6. **Generic openings**: "Thank you for having me" is forgettable — start with substance
7. **Weak closes**: "That's all I have" is anticlimactic — close with purpose
